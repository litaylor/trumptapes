UNC students don't see “locker room talk” as an excuse for Trump's comments about women

Produced by Sharon Nunn, Langston Taylor, Audrey Wells and Erin Wygant

In the wake of Donald Trump’s recent comments about women, more allegations of sexual assault have emerged and a nationwide discussion about sexual assault has dominated the headlines.

These discussions have even made their way into dining room conversation. Ferrel Guillory, a professor in the UNC School of Media and Journalism, said he had a personal experience of how explosive Trump’s comments were going to be early on.

“My children and grandchildren come over for dinner on Friday nights. So at 6:30 I turned on the national news to see what the hubbub was about. And my daughter yelled at me ‘turn it off,’” he said. “She was sitting next to her 14-year-old and he hadn’t seen it yet and she wanted to talk to him about it before.”

He said his first impression was that Trump’s comments were out of bounds and not something you would want a teenager to hear.

In a three-minute clip obtained by the Washington Post, Trump said he can’t resist kissing beautiful women, and said he has special status with them because he is a celebrity.

“When you’re a star, they let you do it. You can do anything,” he said.

Many Republican leaders, such as House Speaker Paul Ryan, Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell, senators Ted Cruz and Marco Rubio, have withdrawn their support, expressing disdain for Trump’s comments.

Guillory said this has raised a question of support for the party in this election.

“They instinctively want to support the candidate of their party. And there’s good reason in the political dynamics of American Politics that you support your party,” he said. “But what Trump has done is raise the question of support for the party as the be all and end all, or whether otherwise ethical, upstanding political figures should renounce their party’s nominee because of the clear defects of their character.”

This question isn’t just for Republican Party leaders. UNC College Republicans have been grappling with the same issue. Hayden Vick, the group’s chairman, said they have a tough job during this election.

“I’d say throughout history you could find examples where Republicans who didn’t like the nominee could find ways to like Democratic nominee. Right now, we’re looking at two nominees who so many in their own parties have disdain for,” he said.

The group hasn’t made an official endorsement because they have members who support Trump as the nominee and others who don’t.

“We want to make sure we don’t isolate members who love him, but we also recognize that many members don’t like him,” Vick said. “At the end of the day, we have an obligation to our members before we have an obligation to the rest of the state or to Donald Trump.”

The executive board of UNC College Republicans denounced Trump’s comments in a statement released after the video was leaked. They said they will continue to fight for conservative values and those who promote them, but they repudiate the comments of the nominee.

“Mr. Trump’s words were, in every way, inadmissible, and we do not make, nor support, any justification on his behalf… There is no excuse, there is no explanation, there is only regret as we distance ourselves from the words of the Republican nominee,” the executive board said in the release.

Vick said he doesn’t agree with the justification that the comments were just “locker room talk.”

“When I hear things like that, it’s not locker room talk. What he was saying was geared a lot more towards sexual assault than it was towards harmless, guy joking,” he said.

Sexual Assault is not an unfamiliar topic for students at UNC. In 2012, Landen Gambill went public with allegations that her ex-boyfriend sexually abused her, and the university treated her unfairly throughout the investigation of her claims.

Gambill and four other women, including a former administrator, filed a Title IX complaint against the university in January 2013. In the complaint, they said the university pressured the former administrator to underreport the number of sexual assaults to the federal government.

UNC was one of the initial 55 colleges under investigation by the Department of Education for Title IX violations in May 2014.

Since, the university created a new policy on discrimination, harassment and related misconduct in order to address changing the misconduct policies related to sexual assault.

But, on a campus that has seen half a decade of intense criticism, allegations against the university and Title IX are still coming forward.

In September, sophomore Delaney Robinson said she survived a sexual assault at an on-campus apartment complex in February 2016.

She said she cooperated by allowing a rape kit to be taken and gave a statement to law enforcement. However she said, the university and the Title IX office have done nothing six months after her assault.

“I did not realize that rather than receiving support and concern from the University, I would only be further victimized by the people who should be working to keep us safe,” Robinson said in a release.

Robinson sought two self-sworn warrants of misdemeanor assault on a female and sexual battery and against Allen Artis, a former UNC linebacker.

After Artis was charged, his lawyer, Kerry Sutton, said Artis couldn’t possibly get a fair hearing in the Title IX process.

“This has been manipulated sadly, poorly, horrifically so that this young man has had the arc of his life altered,” Sutton said in a press conference.

Some students are disappointed with how this case has been handled. Senior Hannah Petersen said this case has a silencing effect because there is tangible proof in this case but nothing has come of it.

“I'm very disappointed in the reaction to this case. Because, unlike so many others, Delaney did everything right,” Petersen said.

Other students said this case presents an idea that athletes have a different set of standards. Senior Caleb Waters said this is an interesting case.

“There is this idea of rape culture among, especially, college football players, and so there's this idea that athletes have a different sort of set of standards than most people do,” Waters said.

Senior Julia Whitfield said the idea of consent should be addressed.

“Because we all understand consent when it means going to the doctor and the doctor's like, 'Read this. I'm going to do this procedure on you,' she said. “And we get that, but we don't see how that can translate when it comes to sexual assault.”

Whitfield also said underage drinking should be addressed. She said underage drinking is commonplace on a college campus, but students should be taught how to drink responsibly and not in excess.

Sexual assault is a very personal issue for many students across UNC’s campus. After Trump’s comments about women, Hannah Beason and a group of her friends got together to create ribbons representing solidarity against sexual assault.

“Every single time we crossed the ribbon, that ribbon could represent one girl that has suffered a sexual assault,” Beason said.

She said Trump’s comments left her without words because it’s hard to make excuses for comments like that.

Senior John Aceti said awareness of rape culture is important. He said if someone knows what perpetuates rape culture or promotes a negative image of women, they can point that out to other people.

Aceti said sometimes people like to brag, and there can be a joking aspect to it, but Trump’s comments aren’t a joke.

“It doesn't make a lot of sense to me for it to be 'locker room talk,' because that's pretty explicit stuff that you don't expect to be heard or said anywhere,” Aceti said.
